How to Save the World, Part 2

This is an overview of the large, ill-defined and potentially-impossible biological engineering project I think could achieve carbon-sequestration on a global and historically significant scale. This is part 2—in part 1 I tried to explain why I feel a project like this is necessary, compared to the more commonly discussed alternatives.

The big picture

The short version is this: engineer a species of oceanic cyanobacteriaSpecifically something from the Prochlorococcus genus so that they fix carbon at a faster rate than wild-type. Recode the organism's genome so that it is entirely genetically isolated, even when released into the wild, without the need for synthetic nutrients. Let it work.

Motivation

The scale of carbon sequestration necessary to abate climate change is enormous, and no human-scale method I know of can approach the level of efficiency that would be necessary. Classical bioengineering approaches are similarly infeasible, as a managed biological solutionBy "managed" I mean trying to sequester carbon by growing a huge amount of biomass on farms. will start to encroach on and compete with agricultural resources. This is untenable because the demand for food will always overrule the need for carbon sequestration. A huge amount of carbon is captured by plankton in the ocean, and accelerating this process is an attractive strategy for sequestration. However, it raises some obvious concerns.

At the necessary scale it is not possible to keep this organism isolated in labs or factories—the infrastructure necessary would be far too expensive and energy-intensive. The only strategy that could plausibly achieve the right scale is if the organism grows freely in the ocean. But releasing a genetically-engineered organism to grow unchecked in the wild is a controversial ideaTo put it mildly!, and for good reason. One of the biggest challenges of this proposal is that we must weigh the plausible risks of the approach against the probable consequences of inaction. For a long time, climate change has been ignored because of the cost of action. At this point we must seriously consider the huge cost of doing nothing.

One major worry is the potential for horizontal gene transfer—any DNA put into bacteria will spread to any other bacteria in the same environment, almost immediately. Below I will outline a genetic-recoding method that will effectively make this impossible, and in a later post I will go into more detail.There's always the chance that I'm overlooking a biological mechanism that would allow transfer, which is why open peer review is useful. I'm confident that this aspect of the proposal is achievable; although a substantial amount of work will be involved, the building blocks have already been demonstrated by others.

Another concern is that such an organism might work too well, removing so much CO2 that global temperatures fall dramatically. I typically refer to this as a Snowpiercer scenario.Which is a bad idea from a PR standpoint but the reference is obvious. This aspect of the project requires a lot more research to resolve, and I will go into the research questions I see in a later post. It's unclear whether the protein engineering necessary is possible at all, to say nothing of whether it could work so well that it becomes a problem.
